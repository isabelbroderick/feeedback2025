In journalism, bias means presenting the news in a way that favors one side or opinion. This can happen through the words a reporter chooses, what facts they include or leave out, and even who they interview.

To spot bias, ask yourself: Whose voices are being heard? What facts are missing? Are opinions being reported as facts? Good journalists aim to show all sides fairly, so that all the facts are there allowing readers to make up their own minds.

Unfinished Article Continued

In news reporting, bias can show up in many ways the most obvious of which is tone. this normally eqates to a positive or negative bias:

Positive bias: Stories that emphasize the good side of events. You’ll see language that highlights achievements, hopeful outcomes, or inspiring stories—sometimes making situations appear better than they really are.

Negative bias: Articles that focus on problems, risks, or failures. The language often stresses conflict, danger, or shortcomings—sometimes making events seem worse or more alarming than they actually are.
